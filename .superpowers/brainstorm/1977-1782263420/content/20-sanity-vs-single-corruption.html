Design Decision: One Corruption Value vs Separate Sanity + Corruption

The big fork you're stuck on. This affects the entire character fantasy (Saint vs Sinner), the orb tree, Church/Guild systems, and how the player feels Limbo.

Option 1: Single "Corruption" Value

One hidden number for the player. It drives both personal effects and world effects.

  • Stacking duplicates in the tree increases the single corruption value.
  • High value makes the player pay more (sanity-style cost to use orbs, personal strain).
  • High value also makes the world/NPCs react (water, shadows, random battles, NPC behavior).
  • Church purification lowers the single value (and weakens the orb).

Pros of Single Value

  • Simpler to track, balance, and explain internally.
  • Strong unified "infection" fantasy — your soul is rotting, so the city rots too.
  • Every stack or action has clear dual consequences.
  • Easier to make the Saint/Sinner choice feel binary and high-stakes.

Cons of Single Value

  • Harder to create interesting mechanical separation between "how I feel" and "how Florence feels".
  • Player might feel punished in two ways for the same choice, with less agency.
  • Church purification has to fix both at once, which can feel blunt.
  • Less room for "player is suffering but the city is still okay" moments.
Option 2: Separate Sanity (Player) + Corruption (World)

Two linked but distinct hidden values.

  • Sanity (player-based): The personal cost. Stacking duplicates primarily increases this. Affects ability use cost, personal debuffs, risk of "losing it", dialogue options, etc.
  • Corruption (world-based): What bleeds into Florence, NPCs, environment (water color, shadows, random battles, NPC suspicion or Limbo behavior).
  • They can move somewhat independently. High sanity doesn't automatically max world corruption.
  • Church can target one or both (purify ability = big sanity drop + smaller world effect).

Pros of Separate Values

  • Clear thematic split: "My soul is breaking" vs "The city is breaking because of me".
  • More interesting gameplay loops and tradeoffs (manage my sanity vs manage the city's corruption).
  • Better support for Saint vs Sinner fantasy. A Saint might sacrifice power to keep personal sanity clean even if the world is still tainted.
  • More levers for systems (Guilds could be better at world corruption, Church at personal sanity, or vice versa).
  • Stacking duplicates can feel like a deliberate "I'm willing to pay personally for power" choice.

Cons of Separate Values

  • More systems to track and balance (two hidden values instead of one).
  • Risk of the player feeling the split is arbitrary if the two values don't clearly interact.
  • Slightly more complex to communicate through pure environmental/player feedback (no numbers).

My Recommendation

Go with separate Sanity (player) + Corruption (world).

This game is already built around a very strong "personal damnation vs city damnation" theme from Dante. A single value makes that theme feel flatter. Separate values let you do cooler things like:

  • Player who is personally "clean" (low sanity cost) but the city is still rotting because of past choices.
  • Player who is personally broken (high sanity cost, aggressive orbs) but has worked with Guilds/Church to keep Florence from completely falling apart.
  • More meaningful Saint vs Sinner branching in the tree and in story beats.
  • Stacking duplicates becomes a deliberate personal sacrifice ("I'm willing to pay with my sanity for this power") rather than just "more corruption everywhere".

The extra complexity is worth it because the fantasy is literally about two different things rotting at different rates.

A

Single Value

One corruption number that hits both player and world. Simpler, more unified infection fantasy.

B

Separate Values

Sanity (player personal cost) + Corruption (world/NPC/environment). More thematic and mechanical depth.

C

Hybrid

One main hidden value, but with separate "personal multiplier" and "world multiplier" that tree nodes and choices can affect differently.
