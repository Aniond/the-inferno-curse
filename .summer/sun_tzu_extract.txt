Sun Tzu and Infernal Curse Game AI
Executive Summary
Sun Tzu’s The Art of War is best treated not as a bag of famous maxims, but as a compact theory of strategic
advantage: shape conditions before combat, win by information superiority, avoid waste, strike weakness
instead of strength, and stay adaptable to terrain, timing, and uncertainty. Modern scholarship also
cautions against reading the text as simple warmongering. Major translators and interpreters—including
Samuel B. Griffith, Roger T. Ames, Ralph D. Sawyer , Victor H. Mair , Michael Nylan, Derek M. C. Yuen, and
Scott Boorman—variously emphasize military pragmatism, archaeological and philological accuracy, Daoist
context, textual layering, literary structure, and the need to separate the text’s original Warring States
context from later analogical uses. That scholarly diversity matters for game AI design: it pushes you away
from “quote-driven” gimmicks and toward systems that create conditions, misdirect attention, preserve
resources, and learn the player’s habits. 
For an infernal curse game, the strongest design move is a hybrid AI architecture rather than a single
technique. The strategic layer should use utility scoring plus lightweight planning to decide whether to
spread corruption, ambush, withdraw, feint, isolate, or escalate. The tactical layer should use behavior trees
and influence maps for reactive execution, positioning, and hazard use. The predictive layer should
maintain a player-belief model and opponent model. The learning layer should be mostly offline—self-play
reinforcement learning, policy tuning, or parameter search—rather than unrestricted online learning, so
that difficulty remains legible and fair . This matches both Sun Tzu’s emphasis on foreknowledge, deception,
timing, and flexibility, and the practical strengths of behavior trees, GOAP , utility systems, opponent
modeling, MCTS, reinforcement learning, and self-play methods documented in game AI and multi-agent
learning literature. 
The infernal curse theme is not just aesthetic dressing; it is a systems opportunity. Curse mechanics are a
natural fit for graph diffusion, threshold effects, cellular-automata spread, hidden infection stages,
territorial pressure, sacrificial rituals, and corrupted intelligence networks. This lets level design, enemy AI,
and progression systems reinforce one another: cursed altars become command-and-control nodes, NPC
afflictions become information leaks or ambush bait, and environmental hazards become dynamic “terrain”
in the Sunzian sense. Network and threshold diffusion models, along with grid-based propagation methods,
offer strong formal templates for curse spread without forcing a single genre or platform assumption. 
The central design risk is that Sun Tzu-inspired AI can become oppressive if it is implemented as invisible
cheating rather than dramatic strategy. The answer is bounded rationality, informational parity, and
explainable intent. Strong infernal AI should deceive, but its deception should be telegraphed; it should
exploit terrain, but only terrain the player can learn to read; it should propagate curses, but through visible
carriers, rituals, reservoirs, and thresholds. Explainable AI research for designers and broader ethics work in
games both support this direction: transparency, trust, and user-centered interpretability are not luxuries,
but part of whether adaptive systems feel fair and narratively meaningful.

Sources and interpretive frame
The text itself is short, aphoristic, and historically layered. The received work has 13 chapters, but modern
scholarship often treats it as an anthology that reached something like its canonical form during the
Warring States period rather than a perfectly unified work written at one sitting by a single historical
mastermind. Mair’s Columbia edition foregrounds archaeologically recovered manuscripts, textual and
contextual problems, and even the possibility that “Sun Wu” is a constructed authorial figure. The
Association for Asian Studies likewise summarizes the mainstream scholarly caution: the work is ancient,
influential, and likely layered, not a transparently authored modern-style treatise. 
That matters for a game designer because there are really three different things one can mean by “using
Sun Tzu”: using the original Warring States text in context, using military ideas historically influenced by Sun
Tzu, or using generalized strategic analogies in modern competitive systems. Scott Boorman’s recent “three
faces” framing is useful here: it separates the original text, later military application, and modern strategic
generalization. For AI design in games, the third face is legitimate only if it remains disciplined by the first
two; otherwise “Sun Tzu” collapses into vague branding.

A few interpretive points are especially load-bearing for your game. First, key scholars resist reading the
book as a celebration of endless combat. Nylan explicitly argues that the text is often misunderstood and is
deeply concerned with the costs of war and alternatives to direct fighting; Mair and his reviewers also stress
the text’s minimalist and psychologically oriented approach. Second, Yuen argues that recovering Chinese
and Daoist context is necessary because otherwise Western readers flatten the work into familiar linear
schemas. Third, Steven Combs’ rhetorical reading of the book highlights parsimony—economy,
indirectness, and minimal waste—as a central logic rather than an incidental one. Together , these
interpretations support a game AI that wins by shaping, economizing, and misdirecting more than by stat
inflation. 
One final caution: translation choices change design thinking. Nylan’s reviewer shows that even a term as
basic as guo can be framed as “state,” “nation,” or “ruling house,” each nudging interpretation in a different
direction. A designer using Sun Tzu for faction AI, cult hierarchy, or sovereign-command narratives should
therefore treat translation as part of the design brief, not mere packaging. 
Strategic principles extracted for an infernal curse game
Several principles are stable across major readings of the text and are directly useful for AI design. Sun Tzu
states that “all warfare is based on deception”; he argues that the highest excellence is to break resistance
without fighting; he treats self-knowledge and enemy knowledge as prerequisites of victory; he insists that
war should avoid protracted waste; he compares tactics to water adapting to ground; he assigns major
importance to terrain, timing, and foreknowledge through spies; and he repeatedly frames prudence as
essential because destroyed states and dead soldiers cannot simply be restored. Those are not just themes;
they are system design requirements. 
For an infernal curse game, these principles translate cleanly into mechanical design. Deception becomes
false threat signaling, decoys, mimic hazards, feints, hidden reinforcements, and delayed curse activations. 
Terrain becomes more than walls and chokepoints: cursed fog, sacrificial circles, blood-slicked corridors,
collapsing sanctums, sanctified safe rooms, and line-of-sight distortion all become strategic geography. 
Timing becomes ritual windows, staggered ambushes, synchronized phase changes, patience states, and
“wait until the player overcommits” logic. Intelligence becomes scouting, noise tracking, memory of the
player’s routes, inference of consumable usage, and infection surveillance. Flexibility becomes policy
switches between hunt, pressure, contain, isolate, and retreat. Economy of force becomes attritional
pressure through the environment and curse spread rather than endless direct assaults. 
The most useful design abstraction is this: Sun Tzu’s AI is not “aggressive AI”; it is condition-shaping AI.
Instead of asking, “How do enemies attack the player harder?” ask, “How do infernal agents manipulate

AI architecture and behavioral design
Because your platform, genre specifics, player count, and stack are unspecified, the safest recommendation
is an engine-agnostic hybrid architecture. Use a reactive execution layer for moment-to-moment
behavior , a deliberative layer for medium-horizon goals, a belief and modeling layer for uncertainty, and
an optional offline learning layer for tuning. This recommendation is consistent with the established
strengths of behavior trees, GOAP , utility systems, opponent modeling, MCTS, and self-play RL rather than
trying to force any one technique to do everything. Behavior trees scale well because they are modular and
reactive; GOAP was explicitly adapted to make planning practical for real-time games; utility systems are
strong when many competing actions must be scored continuously; MCTS is valuable when short-horizon

In practice, I would map the principles to algorithms this way. Use a utility system to score strategic intents
such as ambush now, seed curse reservoir, withdraw behind a sanctum breach, bait the player into a ritual
chamber, or switch to support a stronger demon. Utility curves are especially good at representing partial
desirability rather than hard scripted thresholds. Use behavior trees to execute those intents with tight
reactivity: move, crouch, stalk, cast, retreat, relay, flank, corrupt object, or fake death. Use GOAP or
lightweight forward planning when actions have explicit preconditions and multi-step setup, such as
opening a seal, extinguishing light, then triggering a backspawn. Use influence maps to encode tactical
geography: threat, visibility, sanctity, curse density, noise, player pressure, and retreat safety. Use MCTS
only where action branching is meaningful and bounded—for instance in turn-based tactics, roguelike
encounters, card-combat rituals, or boss duel phases with a small discrete action set. Use reinforcement
learning or population-based tuning offline to optimize weights, cooldown policies, and combo timing,
not to replace the whole authored architecture. Use opponent modeling whenever player habits matter:
favored route, panic-heal threshold, dodge rhythm, objective fixation, stealth preference, or risk tolerance.

A good default control policy for an infernal faction is to separate intent from motion. Intent answers why
the unit acts; motion answers how it enacts the choice without looking stupid. This separation is one of the
cleanest ways to preserve authored tone. “Why” should feel Sunzian and infernal. “How” should feel
physically grounded, with good animation, pathing, and timing. If you later add learning, learn the intent
weights first, not the locomotion substrate. That keeps debugging and fairness manageable. This is also
where designer explainability pays off: you can inspect “ambush score rose because player purity dropped,
torchlight exposed path, and retreat lanes improved,” rather than treating the AI as a black box. 
Infernal curse design patterns
An infernal curse theme gains strategic depth when enemies, NPCs, and spaces are all part of the same
corruption ecology. Instead of thinking in separate buckets—“enemy behavior ,” “status effects,” and “level
hazards”—treat the curse as a distributed control system. The curse should have sources, vectors,

reservoirs, thresholds, and counterforces. Epidemiological network models and threshold models provide
a strong abstract parallel here: local transmission rules can create large, readable cascades, while graph
structure determines how quickly corruption spreads or stalls. Cellular automata are especially suitable
when you want local neighborhood spread across tiles or rooms, while network or graph formulations are
better for faction influence, social infection, or shrine-linked corruption. 
For NPCs, I recommend three broad infernal roles. Carriers spread curse states and misinformation,
turning intelligence into infection. Anchors stabilize infernal territory, buffing enemies and altering terrain
until destroyed or purified. Interpreters read the player and coordinate others—seers, choir-priests,
whisperers, archivists, or husk-commanders. This gives you Sun Tzu’s “spy,” “terrain,” and “timing” principles
in diegetic form. A converted villager who visibly begins whispering routes to cultists is more evocative than
an invisible global alert flag; a blood altar that slowly widens a corruption field is better than a flat area-
denial spell because it creates strategic time pressure the player can understand. 
For enemy factions, the following patterns are especially strong. Feigning weakness can be implemented
as wounded enemies that retreat toward kill chambers; mimic sanctuaries can present partially safe rooms
that become cursed once the player commits; ritual pincer attacks can be scheduled once two linked
altars remain intact; converted scouts can be animals, crows, children, or possessed echoes that reveal
location without immediate combat; cursed reserve doctrine can keep elite infernal units hidden until the
player has spent cleansing resources. Each of these patterns embodies strategic shaping rather than brute
aggression. 
Environmental hazards should likewise be strategic, not merely punitive. Strong hazard families include 
propagating contamination such as hellfire veins, blood runoff, and fungal sigils; conditional hazards
such as braziers that ignite only when a ritual chorus completes; information hazards such as false
footsteps, mirror apparitions, and trapped prayers; and sanctity inversion hazards that convert healing,
light, or noise into curse fuel once the player crosses a threshold. These hazards are most effective when
they combine with influence maps, because the AI can then prefer battlegrounds where curse, cover , and
visibility create asymmetry in its favor .

Implementation guidance
The key implementational choice is whether the AI reasons over a full observable world state or a belief
state. For Sun Tzu-inspired infernal AI, belief state is the better default. “Foreknowledge” in the text is not
magic omniscience; it is structured information gathering. Your AI should therefore maintain explicit
confidence values about player location, objective intent, resource state, and curse susceptibility. This
moves the design closer to scouting and counterplay, and further away from unfair omniscience.

Graph diffusion is better for room-based dungeons, shrine networks, settlements, and objective chains.
Cellular automata are better for tile-local spread such as flames, blight, or sigils crawling along floors.
Thresholds prevent the system from feeling like monotonically accumulating background noise; instead, it

For sensors and perception, the most important principle is to make infernal information channels diegetic.
Do not give enemies perfect knowledge unless the fiction clearly justifies it. Recommended sensors are line
of sight, hearing via event splats, “scent” or corruption trails, anchor-linked shared awareness, and
investigation memory. A seer unit may be allowed belief updates through mirrors or ravens; rank-and-file
husks should usually rely on ordinary senses plus nearby relays. That partition preserves role variety and
fairness. Influence maps should fuse these channels into an actionable spatial summary rather than forcing
every agent to reason directly from raw geometry. 
Reward shaping is appropriate if you use RL for offline tuning, but it should be potential-based whenever
possible so you do not distort the optimal policy. In practice, define potentials for states like player isolated
from light, target inside curse-rich zone, anchor defended, ritual interrupted, or curse reservoir connected to
carrier path. Then shape with the delta in potential rather than arbitrary bonus rewards. This will accelerate
learning while preserving policy validity.

Performance-wise, the most important practical rule is to centralize expensive reasoning. Shared
influence maps, zone-level curse simulation, batched perception updates, and staggered planning ticks
matter far more than micro-optimizing one scoring function. If your game turns out to be a real-time action
title, most agents should live on utility plus BT with sparse planning; if it turns out to be turn-based tactics,
increase planning and MCTS budgets; if it turns out to be a card-battler or squad roguelite, lean harder on
simulation and self-play. The architecture above survives all three with limited rework. 
Balancing, playtesting, and anti-exploit measures
Sun Tzu-inspired AI can easily become too strong in the wrong way. The balancing goal should therefore be 
apparent cunning, not hidden omnipotence. An infernal faction ought to feel coordinated, patient, and
opportunistic, but still bounded by the same spatial and informational world the player inhabits. This is
especially important because strong search methods do not automatically produce enjoyable or believable
play. Research on MCTS agents biased by human gameplay data makes the point directly: high-performing
agents can have emergent styles that are not necessarily human-like or enjoyable unless explicitly tuned. 
A rigorous playtesting program should combine designer review, human playtests, and automated-agent
evaluation. Exploratory agents can help evaluate whether curse-laden levels still support meaningful routes,
whether safe rooms are truly safe, and whether exploration remains engaging rather than collapsing into
hazard paralysis. Play traces and trace-dissimilarity methods can then tell you whether different AI builds
actually produce distinct strategic arcs instead of the same encounter pattern wearing different costumes.

Clustering methods help reveal whether players or AI agents are converging on degenerate solutions such
as “kite around the altar clockwise forever” or “always trigger the mimic sanctuary first.” 
I recommend measuring emergent strategic behavior with a compact dashboard rather than only win rate.
The most decision-useful metrics are these: belief accuracy of the AI’s player-location estimates; 
engagement selectivity, meaning percentage of times the AI attacks from advantage rather than parity; 
curse pressure curve over encounter time; territory conversion rate of safe cells to cursed cells; tactical
diversity entropy over top-level intents; feint success rate; resource-denial efficacy; retreat survival
rate; player route diversity; counterplay latency, meaning how quickly players learn to answer a tactic;
and exploitability, meaning whether testers can farm the same deterministic AI flaw repeatedly. These
metrics create a better strategic picture than generic DPS or damage-taken summaries. 
The anti-exploit layer should be mechanical, not punitive. First, use top-k stochasticity so agents do not
always choose the single highest-scoring legal action. Second, decay AI certainty over time so players can
break contact and reset enemy beliefs. Third, cap coordination bandwidth so infernal factions do not
become a perfect hive mind unless narrative rules explicitly justify that state. Fourth, use counter-scripts
against kiting and doorway abuse, but express them as learned doctrine: reserve flankers, curse spillover
that slowly penalizes static loops, or anchor-driven rerouting. Fifth, rotate tactical templates over runs or
difficulty tiers so one solved exploit does not trivialize the whole game. Sixth, treat boss “mind reading” as a
fiction-labeled mechanic with visible cues, not silent parameter cheating. 
Balancing should also distinguish between local fairness and campaign fairness. Local fairness asks
whether an individual encounter feels readable and killable. Campaign fairness asks whether repeated
exposure to infernal pressure exhausts the player’s options in a way that still allows strategic recovery. Sun
Tzu’s economy principle argues against gratuitous attrition; your campaign systems should therefore keep
at least one recovery axis open—purification, sanctuary restoration, ally rescue, or informational
counterplay—even when the infernal network is strong. Otherwise the AI stops feeling strategic and starts
feeling merely smothering. 
Ethics, fairness, and narrative legibility
Ethically and narratively, the most important rule is that enemy intelligence should not erase player agency.
The ethics literature on AI in games warns that elicitation, sensing, detection, and adaptation each
introduce distinct risks around transparency, privacy, ownership, and manipulation. Even when your AI is
not using personal data outside play, it is still adapting to player behavior inside the game. The design
question is therefore not just “Can the AI infer this?” but “Should this inference be legible, consented to by
the game’s fiction and rules, and answerable by player skill?” 
Explainability should exist at two levels. Designer-facing explainability is mandatory: debug views should
reveal utility weights, chosen intents, belief states, curse-pressure maps, and why a trap was armed or not
armed. Player-facing explainability should be selective and diegetic: audio cues, omen symbols, scout
sightings, altar glow, chant buildup, possession tells, raven flight, map corruption edges, and post-
encounter codex notes. The research agenda around explainable AI for designers is directly relevant here:
the point is not only to expose internals for scientists, but to help creative teams shape understandable
experiences. Explainable agency research adds a complementary point: intelligent agents gain trust when
their decisions can be summarized in human-accessible terms.

“Fair deception” in an infernal curse game is absolutely possible. The enemy may lie, but the game should
not. If a false sanctuary can collapse into an ambush, the player should have learnable evidence:
mismatched iconography, wrong-temperature torchlight, corrupted prayer loops, too-clean geometry, or a
previously established behavior of mimic sanctuaries. If cultists can convert villagers into informants, the
player should be able to detect, prevent, or reverse that process. If the AI predicts route preference, it
should do so because scouts saw the player or because the player repeatedly telegraphed a preference, not
because the simulation peeked into the user’s unobservable future. 
Narratively, this framework supports a strong theme: the infernal curse is terrifying not because it is
arbitrary, but because it is strategic. It studies, corrupts, economizes, and waits. The player wins not by out-
DPSing hell, but by disrupting its intelligence, cleansing its reservoirs, breaking its anchor network, and
forcing it into direct engagements on unfavorable ground. That creates a satisfying consonance between
fiction and mechanics: your infernal world “thinks like Sun Tzu,” and the player learns to beat it by
recognizing and reversing that logic. 
The cleanest final design principle is therefore this: build infernal AI that shapes conditions, not just
damage numbers; then make those conditions readable enough that the player can learn, adapt, and
finally turn the enemy’s own strategy against it. That is the point where Sun Tzu stops being decorative
source material and becomes a real design framework.
